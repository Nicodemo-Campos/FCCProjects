Juan Manuel de Rosas

The Restorer of the Laws.

Portrait of Juan Manuel de Rosas.

The life of Juan Manuel de Rosas is based on a life full of struggles.

  • 1793: Birth of Juan Manuel de Rosas, Juan Manuel José Domingo Ortiz de Rozas and López de Osornio was born on March 30, 1793 in Buenos Aires, capital of the Viceroyalty of Río de la Plata. His birth took place on the land owned by his mother, Agustina López de Osornio, where his maternal grandfather Clemente López de Osornio had lived, located on the street that at that time was called Santa Lucía, now Calle Sarmiento between Florida and Calle Florida. San Martín, in the city of Buenos Aires.
  • 1813: Marriage of Juan Manuel de Rosas with Encarnación Ezcurra. He married Encarnación Ezcurra, with whom he had three children: Juan Bautista, born on July 30, 1814, María, born on March 26, 1816 and died the next day, and Manuela, known as Manuelita and born on May 24. of 1817, who would later be his inseparable companion.
  • 1820: Beginnings in the political participation of Juan Manuel de Rosas. Rosas supported Martín Rodriguez's candidacy for the governorship of Buenos Aires.
  • 1828: Triumph of federalism headed by Juan Manuel de Rosas. After the overthrow and execution of Governor Manuel Dorrego Juan Manuel de Rosas led a popular uprising that triumphed in Buenos Aires and the rest of the coast, while the interior provinces remained in the unitary field. After the unitary general José María Paz was captured, the interior was reconquered and Argentina returned to unity under the aegis of the federalist caudillos Juan Manuel de Rosas, Estanislao López and Facundo Quiroga.
  • 1829: Juan Manuel de Rosas acts as Governor of Buenos Aires. The Buenos Aires legislature proclaimed Juan Manuel de Rosas as Governor of Buenos Aires on December 8, 1829, also honoring him with the title of Restorer of the Laws and Institutions of the Province of Buenos Aires, and in the same act granted him "all the ordinary and extraordinary powers that it deems necessary, until the meeting of a new legislature".
  • 1832: Juan Manuel de Rosas resigns from the government. Rosas resigned because he was not granted absolute powers. He left office to a man he trusted, Balcarce, although Rosas continued to dominate the situation as commander-in-chief of the army.
  • 1835: Juan Manuel de Rosas returns to assume the government of Buenos Aires, Governor of Buenos Aires again, now with full powers.
  • 1837: Juan Manuel de Rosas faces the blockade of the French army and the confrontation between the Peru-Bolivia confederation. With French support, the Unitarian Juan Lavalle organized an army of discontents that advanced towards Buenos Aires. However, Rosas, after achieving a treaty with France, was able to reconquer the interior, where he appointed addict governors.
  • 1842: Juan Manuel de Rosas obtains all power over the National territory. He proclaimed himself and dissolved the House of Representatives. Leaning on the federal masses, he organized the Apostolic Restorer Party and kept the country in a perennial crusade against the Unitarians, exterminating its enemies. His dictatorial government achieved internal political stability, maintained national integrity, and favored economic growth. He intervened in the internal conflicts of Uruguay, supporting the conservative Manuel Oribe against the liberal José Fructuoso Rivera.
  • 1845: Juan Manuel de Rosas blocks Montevideo and comes into conflict with Great Britain and France. It besieged Montevideo, but the British forced the Argentine squad to lift the blockade. Argentina then had to suffer the intervention of the British and the French, who blockaded Buenos Aires and organized an expedition to penetrate Paraná, although the interventionists failed to overthrow Rosas.
  • 1850: Rebellion of Justo Jose Urquiza against Juan Manuel de Rosas. Justo José de Urquiza, Governor of Entre Ríos, rebelled with the support of the Unitarians and the governments of Brazil and Montevideo, invaded Santa Fe, and marched on Buenos Aires.
  • 1852: Juan Manuel de Rosas loses the battle of landlords. Urquiza moved to Santa Fe, overthrew Echagüe there and attacked Rosas. After Pacheco's defection, Rosas assumed command of his army, at the head of which he was defeated in the Battle of Caseros, on February 3, 1852. After the defeat, Rosas left the battlefield - accompanied only by an assistant - and signed his resignation in the "Hueco de los sauces".
  • 1857: Juan Manuel de Rosas is tried, tried and sentenced to death in absentia by the Senate and the House of Representatives. Losing all his influence, he spent the last twenty years of his life in exile.
  • 1877: Juan Manuel de Rosas dies. He died in exile on March 14, 1877 at the age of 83, accompanied by his daughter Manuelita, on his estate in Southampton, England.
